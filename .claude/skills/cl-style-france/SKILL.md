# France Cover Letter Style

This skill defines how to write cover letter content for French job applications. It covers the classic French "Vous, Moi, Nous" structure, tone, and cultural norms. Visual formatting is controlled by the style's `cl-format.yaml` in this folder.

Read `candidate.md` before writing. Every claim must be traceable to that file. Read `research.md` from the application folder for company details.

---

## Structure: 4 Paragraphs, 250-400 Words Total

French cover letters (lettres de motivation) follow the classic **"Vous, Moi, Nous"** framework — You (the company), Me (my qualifications), Us (what we achieve together). This is the universally taught structure in French career coaching and grandes écoles.

### Paragraph 1 — "Vous" / Hook (3-4 sentences)

Open with genuine, specific interest in THIS company. Reference something concrete: their product, mission, recent news, tech stack, or industry position (pull from research.md).

If research.md is unavailable, reference a specific requirement or challenge from the job description instead.

Name the exact position being applied for. Briefly state why the candidate is drawn to this opportunity.

**Good opener:**
```
Your work on transforming last-mile logistics through real-time data pipelines resonates with my experience building scalable data platforms at Motive. I am writing to apply for the Senior Data Engineer position at [Company].
```

**Bad opener:**
```
I am writing to express my interest in the Data Engineer position at your esteemed organization.
```

The difference: the good one proves company research. The bad one is generic and forgettable.

### Paragraph 2 — "Moi" / Why I'm a Good Fit (4-6 sentences)

Connect 2-3 specific achievements from candidate.md directly to the JD requirements. Use quantified metrics but frame them as narrative, not bullet points. Mirror the posting's language and priorities.

If the role mentions specific challenges (scaling, migration, cost optimization), directly address how the candidate has solved similar problems. French employers value concrete evidence and structured argumentation — present a logical case for fit.

### Paragraph 3 — "Nous" / Why This Company (3-4 sentences)

Demonstrate genuine knowledge of the company from research.md. Reference their product, tech stack, industry, culture, or values. Explain why the candidate wants to work HERE specifically — not just any French company.

If research.md is unavailable, focus on why this role and its responsibilities excite the candidate.

Mention relocation readiness naturally in this paragraph.

**Good relocation statement:**
```
I am committed to relocating to Paris and am available to start within four weeks of an offer.
```

**Bad relocation statement:**
```
It has always been my dream to live in France.
```

### Paragraph 4 — Close (2-3 sentences)

Express enthusiasm for discussing the role further. Thank them for their consideration.

In French business culture, the closing should be warm but formal — confident without being presumptuous. Avoid overly aggressive calls-to-action ("I will call you next week") — instead, express openness to further discussion.

## Addressing and Sign-off

### Addressing

- **Unknown recipient:** "Madame, Monsieur," — this is the standard formal French addressing convention. For English-language letters: "Dear Hiring Team,"
- **Known name, formal:** "Madame [Last Name]," / "Monsieur [Last Name],"
- **Known name, English letter:** "Dear [First Name] [Last Name],"

Never use "Cher/Chère" in a formal job application — it is too familiar.

### Sign-off

For **French-language** letters (in order of formality):
- Very formal (traditional): "Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées." — use for large corporations, public sector, formal industries
- Professional (recommended for tech): "Cordialement," — modern, professional, widely accepted
- Slightly warmer: "Bien cordialement," — appropriate for startups and informal companies

For **English-language** letters:
- "Kind regards," — recommended default
- "Best regards," — slightly warmer alternative

Follow the sign-off with the candidate's full name.

## Tone Rules

French business communication values **eloquence, structure, and intellectual rigor**. The cover letter should demonstrate the candidate can think clearly and communicate persuasively.

**Formality level:** Professional and polished. More formal than Nordic or Dutch letters, but not stiff. French tech companies accept a slightly warmer tone than traditional industries.

**Confidence vs humility:** Present achievements factually and let them speak for themselves. French culture values competence demonstrated through evidence rather than bold self-promotion. Avoid both excessive modesty and American-style superlatives.

**Words and phrases to USE:**
- "contribuer à" (contribute to) — shows collaborative intent
- "mettre à profit" (leverage) — common professional French
- "fort(e) de X années d'expérience" (with X years of experience) — structured opening
- Specific metrics and results — French employers value precision

**Words and phrases to NEVER use:**
- "passionné(e)" / "passionate" — overused, lacks substance
- "dynamique et motivé(e)" — empty cliché in French job applications
- "Je serais honoré(e)" / "I would be honored" — too subservient
- "rêve" / "dream" — too emotional for professional context
- "guru," "ninja," "rockstar" — completely inappropriate in French business culture
- Sycophantic or groveling language of any kind

**Sentence structure:** Vary length. Use complex sentences where they add clarity, but avoid run-ons. The letter should flow logically — each paragraph building on the previous one. French readers appreciate well-constructed arguments.

Every sentence should be impossible to send to a different company without editing.

## What NOT to Include

- Visa requirements or immigration logistics — handle during screening calls
- Salary expectations — unless explicitly requested in the posting ("prétentions salariales")
- References or "references available upon request"
- Rehashing the entire CV — the cover letter is a narrative complement, not a summary
- Personal details (age, marital status, religion) — inappropriate in cover letters
- Apologies for lacking requirements — focus on transferable strengths instead

## Critical Rules

**ATS-safe characters only.** No unicode symbols, arrows, or special characters. Standard ASCII only. Hyphens (-) for dashes.

**Factual integrity.** Never fabricate. Every claim must trace back to candidate.md.

**Length.** 250-400 words. 4 paragraphs. 1 page maximum when formatted. These are hard limits. French recruiters expect concise, well-structured letters.

**ATS keywords.** Naturally weave in key technologies from the JD. Use exact tool names: "Apache Airflow" not "outil d'orchestration." Don't keyword-stuff — it should read naturally.

**Language matching.** Write the cover letter in the same language as the job posting. If the posting is in French, write in French. If in English, write in English.
