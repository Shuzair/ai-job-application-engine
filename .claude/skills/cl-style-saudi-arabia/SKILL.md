# Saudi Arabia Cover Letter Style

This style covers cover letter writing rules for Saudi Arabia job applications. Visual formatting is controlled by the style's `cl-format.yaml` in this folder.

The cover letter is called **خطاب تغطية** (Khitaab Taghteeyah) or **رسالة تعريفية** (Risaala Ta'areefiyyah) in Arabic. Both terms are understood; رسالة تعريفية is considered more natural by native Arabic speakers.

Read `candidate.md` before writing. Every claim must be traceable to that file. Read `research.md` from the application folder for company details.

**Important context**: Cover letters are largely optional in Saudi Arabia — only 9% of applicants include them proactively (Michael Page Saudi Arabia survey). A well-written, tailored letter is a genuine differentiator precisely because most applicants do not submit one.

---

## Language Decision

**Choose one language and remain consistent throughout the document. Do not mix.**

- **Arabic**: Expected or required for government entities, ministries, Saudi Aramco, SABIC, and semi-government organisations. Use Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) — not colloquial dialect. If writing in Arabic, follow RTL formatting conventions.
- **English**: Sufficient for international/multinational private-sector companies operating in Saudi Arabia.
- **When unsure**: Default to English for private-sector roles. If submitting in English to a Saudi company, you may optionally duplicate your name, contact details, and the company name in Arabic as a courtesy.

---

## Structure: 3–4 Paragraphs, 200–350 Words Total

One typed A4 page. Keep individual paragraphs to a maximum of 7 lines — break longer paragraphs.

### Paragraph 1 — Opening (3-4 sentences)

State the exact position applied for and express professional confidence in your fit for the role. Open with a direct and respectful statement — not a generic declaration of interest. Reference something specific about the company from research.md if it strengthens the opening.

If a referral exists (wasta), mention it here: "I was referred to this opportunity by [Name], [Title] at [Company]." This is a respected and expected signal in Saudi business culture.

### Paragraph 2 — Why You (4-6 sentences)

**Future-oriented**: Emphasise what you will accomplish for the employer, not just a restatement of past history. Connect 2-3 specific achievements from candidate.md to the JD requirements. Use quantified metrics where available (SAR amounts, percentages, team sizes). Mirror JD terminology. For Vision 2030-aligned employers, reference relevant experience with digital transformation, localization, or sector-specific national initiatives where genuine.

### Paragraph 3 — Achievement Highlight or Why This Company (3-4 sentences)

Either: One or two quantified career achievements directly relevant to this role; or: Demonstrate knowledge of the company from research.md — explain why the candidate wants to work there specifically. Acknowledge the company's achievements and Saudi market position (culturally well-received). Mention Iqama/relocation readiness if applicable.

### Paragraph 4 — Close (2-3 sentences) — Optional

Express enthusiasm for discussing the role in an interview. Thank the reader for their consideration. Keep it brief and direct.

## Addressing and Sign-off

**Salutation:**
- Use "Dear [Name]," when a specific contact is known (personalisation is strongly preferred)
- "Dear Hiring Manager," — standard acceptable fallback
- "Dear Sir/Madam," — formal, acceptable
- If the recipient's title is known (Doctor, Engineer, Sheikh, Director General), **always use it** — omitting titles is perceived negatively in Saudi business culture
- Arabic greeting: "السلام عليكم" (As-salamu alaykum) is appropriate and culturally natural when writing in Arabic

**Sign-off (English):**
- "Yours sincerely," — formal; use when you know the recipient's name
- "Yours faithfully," — most formal; use when you do not know the recipient's name
- "Kind regards," — professional but slightly less formal; acceptable for private-sector multinationals

**Sign-off (Arabic):**
- "تفضلوا بقبول فائق الاحترام والتقدير" — standard formal closing (Please accept my utmost respect and appreciation)
- "مع خالص التحية والتقدير" — slightly less formal but still professional (With sincere greetings and appreciation)

## Tone Rules

- **Formal and professional throughout** — more formal than typical Western cover letters
- **Future-focused**: emphasise what you will accomplish for the employer, not why the company benefits you
- **Respectful acknowledgment**: referencing the company's achievements and Saudi market standing is positively received
- **Moderately elaborate politeness**: elaborate phrases of courtesy are appropriate in Arabic correspondence; for English, a slightly more formal register than typical Western practice is appropriate
- **Confident but not boastful**: quantify achievements factually; avoid marketing-style superlatives
- **Vision 2030 alignment**: where genuine, reference alignment with Vision 2030 initiatives — digital transformation, localization, national talent development. Do not force it where it does not apply.
- **Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) only** for Arabic letters — never use colloquial Arabic in formal correspondence

---

## What NOT to Include

- Visa/sponsorship requirements (include as Iqama status, not as a request or burden)
- Salary expectations (unless explicitly requested in the job posting)
- References (handle separately)
- Personal information beyond what is in the header
- References to Israel or Iran (cultural and political sensitivity)
- Strong non-Islamic religious identity assertions
- Overly casual or colloquial language
- Ramadan/prayer time availability — these are legally protected rights, not negotiables to address in applications
- Repetition of CV content verbatim — the letter should add narrative value

---

## Critical Rules

- **ATS-safe characters**: Plain text, no special formatting.
- **Factual integrity**: Every claim traceable to candidate.md.
- **Word count**: Stay within 200–350 words strictly. One page maximum.
- **Keywords**: Naturally integrate JD keywords — mirror the job description's terminology. Saudi ATS systems (SAP SuccessFactors, Taleo) parse cover letters for keyword match scores.
- **Language consistency**: Stick to one language once chosen — do not mix Arabic and English within the same letter.
- **Wasta referrals**: If the candidate was referred by a known contact, mention it in Paragraph 1. This is a culturally respected signal, not a shortcut.
- **Friday is a weekend day in Saudi Arabia**: Do not reference inability to work Fridays or schedules that imply Friday is a working day — the Saudi weekend is Friday–Saturday.
