# Africa Cover Letter Style

This style covers cover letter writing rules for English-language African job applications (South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, Ghana, Rwanda, Ethiopia). Visual formatting is controlled by the style's `cl-format.yaml` in this folder.

Note: In Nigeria, Kenya, and Ghana, the cover letter is commonly called an **"application letter"** — the same document by a different name.

Read `candidate.md` before writing. Every claim must be traceable to that file. Read `research.md` from the application folder for company details.

---

## Structure: 4 Paragraphs, 300–400 Words Total

Target 300–400 words on one A4 page. South African guides (UCT, CVJury) specify half to one full page; Nigerian guides say 250–400 words. Brevity is valued — do not pad to reach a word count.

### Paragraph 1 — Opening (3-4 sentences)

State the exact position being applied for and where it was found (job board, referral, company website). Express genuine, specific enthusiasm — reference something concrete from research.md (a product, recent initiative, company value, or market milestone). Avoid generic openers like "I am writing to express my interest in the role of..." — open with a hook that demonstrates company awareness.

### Paragraph 2 — Why I'm a Good Fit (4-5 sentences)

Connect 2–3 specific achievements from candidate.md to the JD requirements. Use quantified metrics as narrative — percentages, team sizes, revenue impact, timeframes. Mirror JD keywords naturally in phrasing. Demonstrate impact in context rather than listing skills.

### Paragraph 3 — Why This Company (3-4 sentences)

Demonstrate knowledge of the company from research.md — reference their products, market position, values, or recent developments. Explain why the candidate is drawn to this specific company, not just the role. If applying cross-border, mention relocation readiness here.

### Paragraph 4 — Close (2-3 sentences)

Express enthusiasm for discussing the role further. Refer to the attached CV and invite follow-up. Thank them for their time and consideration. Tone: confident but not presumptuous — "I look forward to discussing..." is appropriate; "I am certain I am the perfect candidate..." is not.

## Addressing and Sign-off

**Addressing conventions:**
- Named contact available → "Dear Ms Naidoo," / "Dear Mr Okonkwo," — use surname with title, not first name
- Name unknown → "Dear Hiring Manager," — preferred; explicitly recommended over "To Whom It May Concern" by all major African career guides
- Government / traditional sector → "Dear Sir/Madam," — still acceptable in public sector applications in Nigeria and Kenya
- Never: "To Whom It May Concern" — deprecated across all markets

**Sign-off:**
- Named addressee → "Yours sincerely," (British convention — technically correct, widely used in SA, Nigeria, Kenya)
- Unnamed addressee → "Yours faithfully," (British letter convention)
- Modern alternative → "Kind regards," (acceptable across all markets; common in Nigerian and Kenyan corporate applications)
- Follow with: full name on one line, phone and email on next line

## Tone Rules

- **Professional yet natural**: This phrasing appears in UCT careers, CVJury SA, and Jobberman Nigeria — formal but not stiff.
- **British English throughout**: organise, labour, centre, recognised, favour, programme. Consistent across all English-language African markets.
- **First person is expected in the cover letter** (unlike the CV): "I led...", "I developed..." is appropriate and expected.
- **Enthusiasm tempered by substance**: Genuine interest is welcome; hyperbole is not. Avoid: "absolutely passionate," "over the moon," "perfect candidate," "dream role."
- **No desperation signals**: Do not mention unemployment status, financial pressures, or urgency.
- **Quantify at least 2 achievements**: Include specific metrics (percentages, sizes, timeframes) to substantiate claims.
- **"Application letter" usage**: If writing for a Nigerian, Kenyan, or Ghanaian employer who uses this term in their posting, it is appropriate to mirror it — the content and structure are identical.

---

## What NOT to Include

- Date of birth, ID number, marital status, gender, religion, tribe, or state of origin — never in a cover letter (SA Employment Equity Act; professional norms across all markets)
- Salary expectations — only if the employer's posting explicitly requests them
- Copies of certificates, qualifications, or supporting documents — attach only if explicitly requested
- Referee names or contact details — these belong in a separate document or on the CV
- A restatement of the full CV — the letter complements the CV, not duplicates it
- Handwritten cover letters — all submission is digital in every researched market; typed and formatted only

---

## Critical Rules

- **ATS-safe characters**: Plain text only — no special characters or custom formatting.
- **Factual integrity**: Every claim traceable to candidate.md.
- **Word count**: 300–400 words. Do not exceed one A4 page.
- **British English**: Consistent spelling throughout.
- **Date format in letter header**: Write the date as "5 September 2025" — day (no ordinal suffix), full month name, year. Left-aligned per British business letter convention.
- **No personal information violations**: Do not include race, gender, religion, DOB, or physical descriptions. Consistent with SA Employment Equity Act principles.
- **Keywords**: Naturally integrate JD keywords — do not stuff.
