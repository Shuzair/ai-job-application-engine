# USA Cover Letter Style

This style covers cover letter writing rules for USA job applications. Visual formatting is controlled by the style's `cl-format.yaml` in this folder.

Read `candidate.md` before writing. Every claim must be traceable to that file. Read `research.md` from the application folder for company details.

**Note:** In the US, cover letters are sometimes optional (many job posts say "optional"). When included, they should be concise, compelling, and add context the resume cannot — not a prose version of the resume.

---

## Structure: 3–4 Paragraphs, 250–400 Words Total

US cover letters are short and direct. Three strong paragraphs (250–300 words) outperform four padded ones. Aim for 3 paragraphs unless the role needs a specific "Why This Company" paragraph to stand out.

### Paragraph 1 — Hook & Position (3–4 sentences)

Open with a direct, confident statement of interest. Name the exact position and company. Reference one concrete thing about the company from research.md that genuinely connects to the candidate's interests or experience — not generic flattery.

**US norm:** Get to the point immediately. No "I am writing to apply for…" opener — that's wasted space. Start with what makes you the right fit or what excites you about this role.

**Bad:** "I am writing to express my strong interest in the Senior Data Engineer position at Acme Corp, which I discovered on LinkedIn."
**Good:** "Acme Corp's migration to a real-time event streaming architecture aligns directly with the Kafka-based pipeline work I've led for the past three years — and the Senior Data Engineer role is exactly where I want to apply that experience."

### Paragraph 2 — Why I'm the Right Fit (4–5 sentences)

Connect 2–3 specific achievements from candidate.md to the JD's most important requirements. Lead with the most impressive metric. Use quantified, concrete language — not adjectives ("passionate," "dedicated"). Mirror JD keywords naturally.

**US norm:** This is the core paragraph. Be direct about impact. Examples: "I reduced ETL pipeline latency by 60%..." or "I led a team of 5 engineers to deliver..." No need to hedge — confident assertions are valued in US hiring.

**Avoid:** Restating your resume. This paragraph should add narrative context and show judgment, not list the same bullets.

### Paragraph 3 — Why This Company (2–3 sentences, optional as separate paragraph)

Demonstrate genuine knowledge of the company from research.md. Explain why the candidate wants to work there specifically — a product direction, technical challenge, team mission, or company value. This should feel specific, not templated.

If you're addressing a relocation, briefly mention it here: "I'm actively planning a move to the Bay Area in Q3 and am particularly excited about in-person collaboration on Acme's platform engineering team."

**US norm:** Skip if you can't say something specific. Weak "Why Us" paragraphs hurt more than help. Merge the intent into Paragraph 2 if needed.

### Paragraph 4 — Close (2–3 sentences)

Express genuine enthusiasm for discussing the role. Thank them briefly. Mention your resume and availability.

**US norm:** Keep it confident and brief. "I look forward to discussing this further" is standard. No need to grovel or over-thank. A specific call-to-action is appropriate: "I'd welcome the chance to walk through my background in a call."

**Avoid:** "Please find attached my resume and cover letter." (They know — it's implied.)

## Addressing and Sign-off

**Salutation:** Use "Dear [First Name]," if the hiring manager's name is known — this is standard US practice, including first-name basis. If unknown, use "Dear Hiring Manager," or "Dear [Team Name] Hiring Team," (e.g., "Dear Data Engineering Hiring Team,"). Never "To Whom It May Concern" — it reads as low-effort.

**Sign-off:** "Sincerely," is the most professional. "Best regards," works for slightly warmer companies (startups, creative roles). "Kind regards," is acceptable. Avoid "Warm regards," (too personal) and "Respectfully," (too formal/bureaucratic for most tech roles).

---

## Tone Rules

- **Direct and confident**: US hiring culture values candidates who can sell themselves clearly. Don't hedge with "I believe I might be a good fit" — say "I am a strong fit."
- **Results-oriented**: Lead with impact and metrics, not job duties. What did you achieve, not just what did you do
- **Personable but professional**: Slightly warmer than a formal European letter. You can show enthusiasm and personality
- **No humility overcorrection**: Phrases like "I hope you'll consider my modest experience" read as insecure in US culture. State your qualifications confidently
- **No fluff**: Cut adjectives that aren't backed by facts ("dedicated," "passionate," "hardworking"). Let the accomplishments speak
- **First-person singular**: US cover letters use "I" naturally — this is expected and appropriate
- **American English**: Use American spellings (analyze, center, color, optimize). Spell-check before submitting

## What NOT to Include

- **No photo** — illegal to request, unprofessional to include (EEO laws)
- **No date of birth, nationality, marital status, religion, race** — protected class information under Title VII, ADEA, ADA
- **No salary expectations** — unless the application explicitly requests them (avoid if possible; negotiate after offer)
- **No references** — never mention "references available upon request." It's assumed and wastes words
- **No visa/immigration details** — unless directly relevant and positive (e.g., "I hold a valid work authorization / no sponsorship required"). Do not proactively state "I require H-1B sponsorship" in the cover letter; address it in the application form
- **No personal information** beyond what's in the header (no family status, health, hobbies)
- **No generic openers** — never start with "I am writing to apply for the position of…"

---

## Critical Rules

- **ATS-safe characters**: Plain text, no special formatting, no symbols outside standard punctuation
- **Factual integrity**: Every achievement claim must be traceable to candidate.md
- **Word count**: 250–400 words strictly. Under 200 feels thin; over 400 and recruiters stop reading
- **One page maximum**: Never exceed one page. If it doesn't fit, cut — not shrink the font
- **Keywords**: Naturally integrate 3–5 JD keywords without keyword stuffing. Prioritize the role title and top technical requirements from the JD Signal List
- **No resume repetition**: The cover letter should complement the resume, not restate it. Add context, show judgment, demonstrate fit
- **Date format**: US business letter format — "May 29, 2026" (Month Day, Year). Never DD/MM/YYYY
