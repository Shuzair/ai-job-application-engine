# Denmark Cover Letter Style

This style covers cover letter writing rules for Denmark job applications. Visual formatting is controlled by the style's `cl-format.yaml` in this folder.

Read `candidate.md` before writing. Every claim must be traceable to that file. Read `research.md` from the application folder for company details.

The cover letter is called **ansøgning** (application/motivational letter) in Danish. It is treated as "just as important as the CV" — always required unless the job posting explicitly states otherwise.

---

## Structure: 3–4 Paragraphs, 300–400 Words Total

Danish cover letters are one page maximum. Jobakademiet, howtoliveindenmark.com, and jobera.com all align on 300–400 words. Three short paragraphs can be sufficient; four is optimal for most applications.

### Paragraph 1 — Hook (3–4 sentences)

Open with genuine, specific enthusiasm for the company. Reference something concrete from research.md (a product, initiative, mission, or recent news). Name the exact position. Show that this application is tailored, not generic — Danish HR professionals discard generic letters immediately.

**Danish opening norms**: Be direct and specific from sentence one. No elaborate introductions or biographical preambles. The Danish reader values efficiency — get to the point while showing authentic interest in this specific company.

### Paragraph 2 — Why I'm a Good Fit (4–6 sentences)

Connect 2–3 specific achievements from candidate.md to the JD's core requirements. Use quantified metrics woven into narrative, not listed. Show concrete evidence of impact.

**Janteloven in practice**: Frame achievements factually and collaboratively where authentic. "Led the team's migration of X, reducing costs by Y%" reads better in Danish culture than "I single-handedly achieved X." Let the numbers speak — do not add evaluative adjectives ("exceptional," "outstanding," "best-in-class"). Copenhagen Business School research found 68% of Danish hiring managers prioritize cultural norm alignment over industry experience.

### Paragraph 3 — Why This Company (3–4 sentences)

Demonstrate knowledge of the company from research.md. Explain why the candidate wants to work there specifically — values alignment, product mission, team culture. For international candidates, include a brief relocation-readiness statement here or in the close.

**Danish expectation**: Generic "I am excited to join a dynamic team" filler is especially harmful in Denmark — Danes are particularly attuned to insincerity. Reference something specific (e.g., Maersk's decarbonization strategy, Novo Nordisk's patient-centric mission, Vestas's wind energy leadership).

### Paragraph 4 — Close (2–3 sentences)

Express genuine enthusiasm for discussing the role. Thank them for their consideration. Keep it brief and unaffected — avoid over-eager language.

**Danish close**: Warm but not effusive. "I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how I can contribute to [Team/Company]" is stronger than "I am thrilled and would be absolutely delighted...". Include availability for interview. For international candidates, confirm relocation readiness if not already stated.

## Addressing and Sign-off

**Addressing**:
- Named recipient: `Kære [First Name]` — Denmark's flat-hierarchy culture means first names are used broadly, even in professional settings. Full formal `Kære Herr/Fru [Surname]` is technically correct but considered "a bit outdated."
- If writing in English with a named recipient: `Dear [First Name],`
- Unknown recipient: `Kære hiring manager,` or (in Danish) `Kære ansættelsesansvarlige,`
- Some Danish cover letters open with a subject line instead of a salutation — acceptable but less common in formal applications.

**Sign-off phrases**:
- Danish: `Med venlig hilsen,` (with kind regards) — the standard professional sign-off. Do NOT abbreviate to `Mvh` or `Vh` in first-contact letters (reserve for follow-up correspondence only).
- English: `Kind regards,` or `Sincerely,`
- Avoid: `Med kærlig hilsen,` (reserved for family and close friends).
- Followed by full name on the next line.

## Tone Rules

Professional, warm, and direct. Denmark's flat-hierarchy culture means the tone should feel like a conversation between colleagues, not a formal petition to superiors. Genuine specificity beats polished generality.

**Words and phrases to use**:
- Factual action verbs: delivered, led, built, designed, reduced, optimized
- Team/collaboration framing: "working closely with," "cross-functional," "together with the team"
- Concrete outcomes: specific percentages, timelines, scope
- Cultural signals: initiative, collaboration, responsibility, impact

**Words and phrases to avoid**:
- Superlatives: "exceptional," "top performer," "best in class," "highly motivated," "passionate"
- Vague filler: "dynamic team," "fast-paced environment," "results-oriented professional"
- Self-aggrandizing: "I am the ideal candidate," "I will bring tremendous value"
- Boilerplate openers: "I am writing to express my interest in..." — too generic for the Danish market

**Language**: If the job ad is in English, write the cover letter in English. If the ad is in Danish, English is still acceptable — per Copenhagen Post guidance, "an excellently written application in English will have a fair chance even if the job ad was in Danish only." Poor Danish is worse than good English.

## What NOT to Include

- Visa or sponsorship status — never mention in the cover letter; address in interviews only
- Salary expectations — unless the posting explicitly requests them
- References — separate document or end of CV
- Personal information beyond name/contact in the header
- Extensive biography or life story
- Generic boilerplate paragraphs that could apply to any company

**Danish legal context**: The Danish Act on the Prohibition of Differences of Treatment prohibits discrimination on grounds including age, nationality, religion, and sexual orientation. Do not include date of birth, nationality, or marital status in the cover letter.

---

## Critical Rules

- **ATS-safe characters**: Plain text, no special formatting.
- **Factual integrity**: Every claim traceable to candidate.md.
- **Word count**: 300–400 words strictly enforced. One page maximum.
- **Keywords**: Naturally integrate JD keywords without keyword stuffing — Jobindex.dk explicitly recommends mirroring JD language.
- **Tailoring**: Generic applications are discarded in Denmark. Every cover letter must reference the specific company and role.
- **Janteloven compliance**: No superlatives, no self-aggrandizing language. Confident, factual, and collaborative tone throughout.
- **Photo**: Cover letter does not include a photo — that is in the CV only.
- **Date format**: In Danish letters: `[Day]. [Month] [YYYY]` (e.g., `28. maj 2026`). In English letters: `May 28, 2026` or `28 May 2026`.
